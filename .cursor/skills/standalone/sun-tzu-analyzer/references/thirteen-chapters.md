# The 13 Chapters of Sun Tzu's Art of War — Modern Business Mapping

This reference maps each chapter of The Art of War to modern business, career, and trading contexts. Used by the Sun Tzu Competitive Analyzer to ground strategic verdicts in specific principles.

---

## Chapter 1: Laying Plans (始計 / 시계)

**Core Principle**: The outcome of every conflict is determined before it begins — by planning, calculation, and honest assessment of five fundamental factors.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Pre-launch competitive assessment before entering a market
- Due diligence before M&A, partnerships, or major investments
- Strategic planning cycles where assumptions are tested against reality
- Calculating unit economics before scaling

**Key Laws**:
- "All warfare is based on deception" → Competitive positioning is never what it appears; read the real incentive structure
- "The general who wins makes many calculations in his temple before the battle is fought" → Run the numbers before committing resources

**When to Cite**: Use when the user has not assessed the situation thoroughly, or when hasty action is the primary risk.

---

## Chapter 2: Waging War (作戰 / 작전)

**Core Principle**: Prolonged conflict drains resources. Speed and decisive action preserve strength.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Cash burn rate during competitive wars
- The cost of delayed product launches
- Protracted negotiations that erode leverage
- Price wars that destroy margin for all participants

**Key Laws**:
- "There is no instance of a nation benefiting from prolonged warfare" → Extended competitive battles destroy value; win fast or withdraw
- "Bring war material with you from home, but forage on the enemy" → Use the competitor's assets against them (talent, distribution, partnerships)

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is in a war of attrition, bleeding resources, or when speed is the decisive advantage.

---

## Chapter 3: Attack by Stratagem (謀攻 / 모공)

**Core Principle**: The supreme art is to subdue the enemy without fighting. Win through strategy, not force.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Partnerships that neutralize competitors
- Standards and ecosystem lock-in that make competition irrelevant
- Acquisitions that remove threats before they materialize
- Regulatory moats and IP strategies

**Key Laws**:
- "The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting" → The best competitive move eliminates the need to compete
- "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles" → Self-awareness + competitive intelligence = strategic clarity

**When to Cite**: Use when direct confrontation is expensive and indirect strategies (partnerships, ecosystem plays, regulatory positioning) offer a better path.

---

## Chapter 4: Tactical Dispositions (軍形 / 군형)

**Core Principle**: First make yourself undefeatable, then wait for the enemy to make themselves defeatable.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Building defensible moats before expanding
- Strengthening unit economics before growth
- Securing key talent and IP before competitors notice
- Financial resilience (cash reserves) before aggressive moves

**Key Laws**:
- "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat" → Shore up weaknesses before attacking
- "One may know how to conquer without being able to do it" → Having a strategy is not the same as having the capability to execute it

**When to Cite**: Use when the user wants to attack but is not yet in a defensible position, or when patience and preparation are the winning moves.

---

## Chapter 5: Energy (兵勢 / 병세)

**Core Principle**: Build potential energy through preparation, then release it at the decisive moment with overwhelming force.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Product launch timing — building features quietly, then launching with full force
- Marketing campaigns that build anticipation before release
- Negotiation leverage accumulated through alternatives and walk-away power
- Talent accumulation before competitive expansion

**Key Laws**:
- "In battle, use a direct attack to engage and an indirect attack to win" → The visible action is never the real strategy
- "The energy of moving water can wash away boulders — such is the force of momentum" → Momentum, once established, is nearly unstoppable

**When to Cite**: Use when the user needs to build momentum before releasing, or when they are trying to force an outcome without sufficient accumulated energy.

---

## Chapter 6: Weak Points and Strong (虛實 / 허실)

**Core Principle**: Strike where the enemy is not prepared. Avoid their strength; attack their emptiness.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Targeting underserved market segments competitors ignore
- Attacking weak product categories rather than competing head-on in core offerings
- Geographic or demographic flanking
- Timing moves when competitor attention is elsewhere (during their own product launches, leadership transitions)

**Key Laws**:
- "Strike where he is not" → The best competitive move targets the gap, not the stronghold
- "Be extremely subtle, even to the point of formlessness" → Competitive moves should be unreadable until it is too late

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is considering direct confrontation, and a flanking move through gaps would be more effective.

---

## Chapter 7: Maneuvering (軍爭 / 군쟁)

**Core Principle**: Control the conditions of engagement. Make the long route short and the short route dangerous for the enemy.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Platform strategy that forces competitors to play on your terms
- API/integration strategy that creates switching costs
- Controlling the narrative through thought leadership and media
- Channel partnerships that alter the competitive landscape

**Key Laws**:
- "Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt" → Move with decisive speed once conditions are set
- "Turn the devious into the direct" → Transform disadvantages into advantages through creative maneuvering

**When to Cite**: Use when the user needs to change the terms of competition rather than competing on the enemy's terms.

---

## Chapter 8: Variation in Tactics (九變 / 구변)

**Core Principle**: Rigid plans lose. Adapt to changing conditions. Predictability is vulnerability.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Pivot readiness — ability to shift strategy based on market feedback
- Multiple contingency plans for different competitive responses
- A/B testing and rapid iteration over fixed roadmaps
- Reading competitor signals and adjusting before they expect it

**Key Laws**:
- "Do not repeat the tactics that gained you one victory, but let your methods be regulated by the infinite variety of circumstances" → What worked last quarter may be fatal this quarter
- "There are roads which must not be followed, armies which must not be attacked" → Some battles are not worth winning

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is being predictable, or when they are applying a previously successful playbook to a changed environment.

---

## Chapter 9: The Army on the March (行軍 / 행군)

**Core Principle**: Read the signals your opponent is sending — their movements reveal their intentions and vulnerabilities.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Competitor signal reading (hiring patterns, patent filings, press releases, job postings)
- Customer churn patterns as early warning signals
- Market data interpretation (volume, sentiment, engagement metrics)
- Reading organizational signals (leadership changes, reorgs, cultural shifts)

**Key Laws**:
- "When the enemy's envoys speak in humble terms while he continues his preparations, he will advance" → Watch what they do, not what they say
- "Peace proposals unaccompanied by a sworn covenant indicate a plot" → Partnership overtures from competitors deserve skepticism

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is not reading signals correctly, or when competitor behavior reveals hidden intent.

---

## Chapter 10: Terrain (地形 / 지형)

**Core Principle**: The nature of the ground determines the strategy. Know what ground you are on and what it demands.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Market structure analysis (winner-take-all vs. fragmented)
- Platform vs. product dynamics
- Regulatory and compliance landscape
- Network effects and ecosystem dependencies

**Six Types of Terrain (Business Adapted)**:
1. **Accessible** — Open market, easy entry → Move first
2. **Entangling** — Easy to enter, hard to retreat → Commit only with advantage
3. **Temporizing** — Neither side should move first → Wait for the opponent to commit
4. **Narrow passes** — Control the chokepoint → Lock down distribution channels
5. **Precipitous heights** — High ground advantage → Establish category leadership
6. **Positions at distance** — Equal standing → Do not engage directly, find asymmetry

**When to Cite**: Use when the user does not understand the structural constraints of their competitive environment.

## Chapter 11: The Nine Situations (九地 / 구지)

**Core Principle**: There are nine strategic positions. Each demands a different response. Misidentifying your position leads to the wrong move.

**Nine Positions (Business Adapted)**:
1. **Dispersive** — Fighting on home turf → Unite, do not fragment effort
2. **Facile** — Just entered enemy territory → Do not stop, push forward
3. **Contentious** — Ground both sides want → Secure it, but do not fight for it head-on
4. **Open** — No advantage either way → Maintain flexibility
5. **Intersecting** — Multiple stakeholders → Build alliances first
6. **Serious** — Deep in enemy territory → Live off the land (use competitor assets)
7. **Difficult** — Hard-to-navigate conditions → Keep moving, do not get stuck
8. **Hemmed-in** — Surrounded by constraints → Use tactics, not force
9. **Desperate** — No retreat possible → Fight with total commitment

**When to Cite**: Use when the user needs to correctly identify which type of strategic position they occupy, as each demands a fundamentally different response.

---

## Chapter 12: The Attack by Fire (火攻 / 화공)

**Core Principle**: Use force multipliers — indirect methods that amplify the impact far beyond the direct effort invested.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Viral marketing and word-of-mouth amplification
- Leveraging media and PR as force multipliers
- Open-source strategies that recruit the community as allies
- Data and analytics as competitive weapons
- Strategic leaks and controlled information releases

**Key Laws**:
- "Move not unless you see an advantage; use not your troops unless there is something to be gained" → Never deploy force multipliers without clear strategic purpose
- "A fire must be lit at the right time and the right place" → Timing of force multipliers is everything

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is relying solely on direct effort and ignoring available force multipliers.

---

## Chapter 13: The Use of Intelligence (用間 / 용간)

**Core Principle**: Information is the real weapon. Superior intelligence enables all other strategies.

**Modern Business Mapping**:
- Competitive intelligence programs
- Customer research and user interviews
- Data analytics and business intelligence
- Industry analyst relationships
- Advisory board and expert network cultivation
- Open-source intelligence (OSINT) on competitors

**Five Types of Intelligence (Business Adapted)**:
1. **Local** — Industry insiders with operational knowledge
2. **Internal** — Former employees or partners of competitors
3. **Converted** — Competitors' allies turned into your sources
4. **Expendable** — Deliberate disinformation planted in the market
5. **Living** — Ongoing intelligence sources (analysts, advisors, customers)

**Key Laws**:
- "What enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer is foreknowledge" → The side with better information wins
- "Foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits, nor by analogy, nor by calculation. It must be obtained from men who know the enemy situation" → Data from the field beats theory

**When to Cite**: Use when the user is making decisions without adequate intelligence, or when information acquisition should precede action.
